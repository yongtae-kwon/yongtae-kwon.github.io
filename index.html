Working Papers

Green Plates and Red Flags: Effect of Public Shaming with License Plates on Tax Evasion

[Paper]

Detecting and enforcing tax evasion through the misuse of corporate tax benefits is difficult and prohibitively costly for tax authorities. I study South Korea’s recent li- cense plate reform that mandates a distinctive, green license plate for corporate regis- tered cars above a price threshold, introduced to deter individuals purchasing luxury vehicles under corporate registration. I interpret this policy through a social signaling model where the colour and visibility of license plates signal the likelihood and degree of tax evasion. By introducing a visible marker tied to a price threshold, the reform increases both the observability and informational content of this signal. To evaluate the policy, I estimate an event-study design that encompasses the universe of new ve- hicle registrations. Sales of new corporate-registered cars above the threshold decline immediately after the reform, suggesting an initial deterrence effect. However, sales gradually revert to pre-policy levels within 8 to 10 months. Accounting for anticipa- tion effects, the policy reduced 9,401 luxury corporate vehicles through the first eight months, with limited effect afterwards. These findings suggest that policies relying on socially interpreted signals with uncertain informational content may have limited effectiveness in reducing tax evasion.
